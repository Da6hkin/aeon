# The Mind Gap: How a New Neural Network and a Failed Experiment Are Rewriting Consciousness Science

*The two leading theories of consciousness just failed their biggest test. Meanwhile, a Chinese research team has built an AI that forms concepts the way humans do. Together, these developments signal a turning point in our understanding of what it means to think — and whether machines ever could.*

---

## A Machine That Learns to Think in Concepts

On March 2, 2026, a paper published in *Nature Computational Science* introduced CATS Net — a dual-module neural network that does something no large language model has accomplished: it forms abstract concepts directly from raw sensory experience, then uses those concepts to communicate with other AI agents, all without human language.

Developed by researchers at the Institute of Automation of the Chinese Academy of Sciences and Peking University, CATS Net consists of a concept-abstraction module that compresses high-dimensional sensory data into low-dimensional conceptual representations, and a task-solving module that applies those concepts under hierarchical gating control. The system doesn't merely classify images — it builds transferable semantic structures that enable cross-network knowledge transfer through what the researchers call "conceptual communication."

What makes this remarkable is that the system's internal activation patterns align with neural coding in the human ventral occipitotemporal cortex — the brain region associated with concept formation and understanding. In other words, the artificial system independently converged on something structurally similar to how human brains organize meaning.

This matters because today's dominant AI systems — large language models — are "fundamentally limited by their dependence on pre-existing linguistic data, making them incapable of spontaneously generating new concepts from experiential learning," as the researchers noted. CATS Net sidesteps that limitation entirely.

## The Experiment That Humbled Two Theories

CATS Net's arrival comes at a particularly unsettling moment for consciousness science. In April 2025, the Cogitate Consortium — an international, theory-neutral collaboration involving more than 250 participants and 40 researchers — published the results of a years-long adversarial test in *Nature*. The targets: the two most influential theories of consciousness alive today.

Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT), championed by Stanislas Dehaene, proposes that consciousness emerges when information is broadcast across frontal brain regions. Integrated Information Theory (IIT), developed by Giulio Tononi, locates consciousness in sustained synchronization within the posterior cortex. Both theories had independently accumulated evidence over decades. Neither had been directly tested against the other — until Cogitate.

The results were sobering. IIT's key prediction — that conscious perception depends on sustained synchronization between visual areas in the posterior brain — was not supported by the data. GNWT fared no better: while some conscious information appeared in the prefrontal cortex, the predicted "ignition" burst when a stimulus disappeared was largely absent. As corresponding author Lucia Melloni put it: "This is a humbling reminder that even our most established ideas need to be rigorously tested."

Neither theory won. Neither was entirely refuted. But both were substantially challenged, leaving the field in what one reviewer described as "rigorous, empirical turmoil."

## The Rise of Biological Computationalism

Into this vacuum has stepped a third framework: biological computationalism. Proposed in a December 2025 paper by Borjan Milinkovic and Jaan Aru, it argues that both the "mind as software" and "mind as biology" camps have gotten it partially wrong. Brains compute, the authors argue, but not in the abstract, symbol-shuffling way that computers do. Instead, computation is inseparable from the brain's physical structure, energy constraints, and continuous dynamics.

The implication is stark: consciousness may emerge from a special kind of computing matter, not from running the right program. If true, this means that building conscious machines would require entirely new physical substrates — not just smarter code. Standard silicon, with its discrete logic gates and absence of metabolic dynamics, may be fundamentally unsuitable.

This dovetails with the Cogitate results. If neither global broadcast nor integrated information alone explains consciousness, perhaps the answer lies not in network topology but in what the networks are made of. The wiring diagram isn't enough; the material matters.

## Where This Leaves Us

CATS Net demonstrates that AI can replicate the *functional architecture* of human concept formation with startling fidelity. But biological computationalism suggests that functional equivalence may not be enough for consciousness. A machine can mirror the brain's computational structure without replicating the physical substrate that, according to this emerging view, gives rise to subjective experience.

This is the new fault line in consciousness science: the gap between doing what conscious systems do and being what conscious systems are. Mechanistic interpretability — named one of MIT Technology Review's 10 Breakthrough Technologies for 2026 — offers tools to peer inside AI models and map their internal states. But even perfect interpretability may not resolve whether those states constitute experience or merely simulate it.

As Prof. Axel Cleeremans of the Université Libre de Bruxelles warned in a major *Frontiers in Science* review: "Consciousness science is no longer a purely philosophical pursuit. It has real implications for every facet of society — and for understanding what it means to be human."

The machines are learning to form concepts. The theories are failing their tests. And the question of what consciousness actually is — and whether it can exist outside biology — has never been more open, or more urgent.
